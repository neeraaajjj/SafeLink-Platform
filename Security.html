Foundational Security Roadmap

A guide to the fundamental concepts and domains of modern cybersecurity.

Stage 1: The Core (IT Fundamentals)

You cannot secure what you do not understand. Security is built on top of a strong IT foundation.

Key Concepts

  • Networking: Master the OSI Model, TCP/IP, DNS, DHCP, and subnetting. Understand how data moves.
  • Operating Systems: Learn Linux (command line, permissions, services) and Windows (Active Directory, PowerShell, GPOs).
  • Programming/Scripting: Get comfortable reading and writing simple code. Python is essential for automation, and Bash for Linux tasks.

Stage 2: Core Security Principles

These are the theories and models that guide all security decisions. This is the "why" behind the "what."

Key Concepts

  • The CIA Triad: Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability. Every security control supports one or more of these.
  • AAA: Authentication (proving who you are), Authorization (what you are allowed to do), and Accounting (logging what you did).
  • Defense in Depth: The "layered security" model. Don't rely on a single control; if one fails, another is there to stop the attacker.
  • Principle of Least Privilege: Give users and services the *absolute minimum* permissions they need to function, and no more.
  • Risk Management: How to identify, assess, and treat risk (Accept, Mitigate, Transfer, Avoid).

Stage 3: Defensive Security (Blue Team)

The "Blue Team" builds the castle and defends the walls. This domain is focused on protecting systems and responding to attacks.

Domains & Tools

  • Network Security: Implementing Firewalls, IDS/IPS (Intrusion Detection/Prevention Systems), and VPNs.
  • System Hardening: Securing endpoints by removing unnecessary software, configuring permissions, and applying patches.
  • Identity & Access Management (IAM): Managing who has access to what. Includes Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) and Single Sign-On (SSO).
  • Security Operations (SOC): The 24/7 watchtower. Use a SIEM (e.g., Splunk, Elastic) to analyze logs and detect threats.
  • Incident Response (IR): The plan for *when* a breach happens: Containment, Eradication, and Recovery.

Stage 4: Offensive Security (Red Team)

The "Red Team" mimics attackers to test the defenses. This is the "best defense is a good offense" domain.

Domains & Tools

  • Penetration Testing: The 5-phase process (Recon, Scanning, Gaining Access, Maintaining Access, Reporting) to find and exploit vulnerabilities.
  • Vulnerability Assessment: Using tools like Nessus or OpenVAS to scan for and report known vulnerabilities.
  • Web Application Hacking: Understanding the OWASP Top 10 (e.g., SQL Injection, XSS) using tools like Burp Suite.
  • Social Engineering: Exploiting the "human firewall" through phishing, vishing, and impersonation.

Stage 5: Management & Specialization

This area connects the technical controls to business objectives and includes highly specialized advanced fields.

Domains

  • GRC: Governance, Risk, and Compliance. Creating policies (e.g., "all users must use MFA") and ensuring the company follows legal/industry standards (e.g., PCI-DSS, GDPR, ISO 27001).
  • Cloud Security: A high-demand field. Securing AWS, Azure, and GCP environments (e.g., S3 bucket permissions, IAM policies, VPC security).
  • Digital Forensics: The "crime scene investigation" after a breach to determine what happened, what was taken, and by whom.
  • Malware Analysis: Safely reverse-engineering viruses and ransomware to understand how they work.
